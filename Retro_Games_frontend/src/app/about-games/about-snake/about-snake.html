How to play

To start playing, press the green “Play” button.
If you are in the middle of a game and don't like what you see, press the green button again — now labeled “Restart”.
If you have just lost the game and want to try again, press “Play Again” (yes, it's the same green button).
To return to the first page of the game, press the red “Back” button.
If you want to take a break — relax, drink a coffee, and then continue playing to beat your high score — press the orange “Pause” button to stop, and “Resume” to continue.
If you are tired of the game, simply press the blue “Logout” button.

Moving the snake is simple: just press one of the arrow keys to set its direction.
If you press the Up arrow, the snake will move up.
If you press the Down arrow, it will move down.
If you press the Left or Right arrow, the snake will move in that direction.

Rules

The rules are also simple:
You have to eat the apple to earn points. It’s necessary to score a lot of points to be among the top five on the leaderboard.
If you play on Easy, Medium, or Hard mode, you will get 1, 2, and 3 points per apple eaten, but you cannot go through walls.
If you play in Zero Wall mode, you will gain 1 point per apple, but you can go through walls.
You lose when you hit yourself or when you hit the wall (in the first three levels).

History of the game

The Snake genre originated in 1976 with the arcade game Blockade, created by Lane Hauck and Ago Kiss and published by Gremlin Industries. It inspired many clones and adaptations: Bigfoot Bonkers (1976), Atari's Dominos and Surround (1977), Checkmate (1977), and Snafu (1982).

The first home computer version, Worm, appeared in 1978 for the TRS-80. Later came Snake Byte (1982), Nibbler (1982), and Tron (1982), which popularized the light-cycle variant.

During the 1990s, Microsoft included Nibbles with MS-DOS (1991) and released Rattler Race (1992).

The genre achieved global fame in 1998 with the launch of Snake on the Nokia 6110, followed by multiple sequels (Snake II, Snake EX, Snake Xenzia, etc.).

Later years saw new adaptations: Nimble Quest (2013), Snake Rewind (2015), Snake Core (2020), PAKO Caravan (2020), and MageTrain (2025). Online versions like Slither.io (2016), Snake.io (2016, relaunched on Apple Arcade in 2023 and Netflix/Nintendo Switch in 2024), and Snaky Cat (2025) kept the genre alive.

Google also integrated Snake into its apps, such as YouTube (2010), browser doodles (2013), Google Maps (2019), and Chrome (2019). In 2025, Google relaunched Doodle Snake for the Chinese Year of the Snake.

In education, the open-source project Snakeleev (2025) adapted Snake gameplay to teach the periodic table. The concept also influenced media, like a 2025 season of Jet Lag: The Game. Snake remains widely used to teach programming, especially with Python and Pygame.
